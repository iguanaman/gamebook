# Licensed IP Story Ideas

Story premises drawn from well-known licensed worlds, framed for this engine: branching YAML scenes, stats and flags as connective tissue, narrative fidelity over simulation. None of these would be commercial — they're fan-fiction-shaped, the way most great gamebooks of the 80s were. The point is the *shape* of the story, not the legal lane.

Each idea names the world, the angle, and the shape it would take as a gamebook. Pros are about reader experience and fit with the engine's strengths; cons are about authoring weight, IP awkwardness, or where the format strains.

## Star Wars

### Smuggler's Run on Tatooine
Play a freighter pilot who picks up the wrong cargo on Mos Eisley. One night, one city, branching escapes through cantinas, podrace stands, and Hutt palaces. Stats: *Cred*, *Heat*, *Ship Condition*.
- **Pros:** A single planet, a single night — exactly the Fighting Fantasy unit-of-story shape. Familiar geography means you can lean on reader memory instead of describing every dune.
- **Cons:** The IP brings expectations (cameos, lightsabers) that pull toward fan-service over discipline.
- complexity 2

### Imperial Defector
A young officer stationed on a Star Destroyer realises something is wrong. Slow-burn moral story across shifts, briefings, hangar bay encounters. Flags track who you've spoken to, what you've signed, what you've reported.
- **Pros:** Disco Elysium territory — the choices are about who you become, not what you fight. Stats could be *Loyalty* and *Doubt*. The world is constrained (one ship) so authoring stays bounded.
- **Cons:** Demands strong character writing; the ship setting risks repetition without distinct scene voice.
- complexity 3

### Jedi Padawan, Last Days of the Republic
You're 14, on a fact-finding mission with your master when Order 66 is announced. Run, hide, or fight. Branching survival across one planet over several days.
- **Pros:** Built-in stakes, built-in tragedy, clean time pressure. Lone Wolf-style stat gates (*Force Sense*, *Lightsaber Form*) reward prior choices without simulation.
- **Cons:** Force powers want to be a system; resisting that and keeping them as flag-gated narrative beats takes discipline.
- complexity 3

## Dungeons & Dragons

### Waterdeep: One Night in the Yawning Portal
You arrive at the famous tavern with a map, a debt, and three hours before sunrise. NPCs at every table, factions at every door. Hub-and-spoke structure with the tavern as home base.
- **Pros:** Pure Fabled Lands hub geometry. The setting is dense with named characters, so a small scene count goes a long way. *Coin*, *Reputation with Harpers/Zhentarim* as flags.
- **Cons:** D&D readers expect dice and classes; presenting it as pure narrative may feel thin to some.
- complexity 2

### The Mind Flayer's Apprentice
You wake up in an Underdark cell, partially mind-controlled. Each scene has dual narration — what you see and what the flayer wants you to see. Some choices are gated by *Will* recovery flags.
- **Pros:** Rich conditional-text territory — the same scene reads differently depending on resistance flags. Sunless Sea-style intangible inventory (memories, real names, half-truths) maps perfectly.
- **Cons:** Dual narration is hard to author cleanly; risks confusing the reader.
- complexity 4

### A Tomb Robber's Memoir
Framing device: an old thief recounts the heist that ended their career. The reader makes choices for the younger self while the older narrator interjects. Single dungeon, one descent, no resets.
- **Pros:** Constrained scope, classic dungeon-crawl beats translated to pure narrative. The framing lets failure-branches feel like consequence, not game over.
- **Cons:** Dungeon mapping wants to creep into stats; needs strict authorial restraint.
- complexity 3

## Lord of the Rings / Tolkien

### A Hobbit of the Northfarthing
You are not Frodo. You are a hobbit in a quiet village in the year of the War of the Ring, hearing distant news. Domestic stakes that quietly turn serious. Stats: *Standing in the Shire*, *Pipe-weed reserves*.
- **Pros:** The whole engine's "every scene rewards a visit" principle aligns with cosy Shire texture. The big plot is offstage — a perfect contrast device.
- **Cons:** Risk of feeling slight; needs an event that gives weight without dragging in Sauron directly.
- complexity 2

### The Last Patrol of the Rangers
A Dúnedain ranger walking the borders of Eriador, encountering travellers, wraiths, and old ruins. Episodic — each scene a small encounter, with state carrying between them.
- **Pros:** Episodic shape fits scene-as-short-story (Sunless Sea inspiration). Long arc through flags: who you helped, who remembers you.
- **Cons:** Episodic can feel directionless without a strong throughline; needs an end-state arc to give shape.
- complexity 3

## Warhammer 40,000

### Hive City Investigator
You're an Adeptus Arbites detective in a single underhive precinct. A murder, three days, branching interrogations. Bleak, claustrophobic, no aliens — just people.
- **Pros:** 40K's strength is *texture and oppression*, which is all writing, not mechanics. A police procedural shape gives clear scaffolding.
- **Cons:** Tone is unrelentingly dark; not all readers will want that register for a whole story.
- complexity 3

### Imperial Guardsman, First Day
Your regiment lands on a contested world. You don't survive long unless you make good choices. Episodic short scenes, high lethality, stats that decay (*Rations*, *Sanity*, *Lasgun Charge*).
- **Pros:** Fighting Fantasy lethality plus 40K's grim register; failure as branch, not reset, lands hard here.
- **Cons:** Constant death pressure can make the engine's undo system feel like cheating; needs an authoring stance on that.
- complexity 3

## Harry Potter

### A Year at Beauxbatons
Set in the French wizarding school during the same era as the books — the main plot is a rumour from across the Channel. School year structure: terms, exams, the Yule Ball as a mid-point.
- **Pros:** Calendar structure gives clean act breaks. Social flags (friendships, rivalries, professor opinions) drive late-game branching.
- **Cons:** School-year scope can balloon; needs hard scoping (one term, one year max).
- complexity 4

### The Auror's Apprentice
Post-Voldemort, you're rookie at the Ministry investigating residual Death Eater cells. Casework structure: each case is a self-contained arc, flags carry forward.
- **Pros:** Episodic case structure is exactly the Fabled Lands hub model. Each case is a writable unit.
- **Cons:** Demands consistent procedural writing voice across many cases.
- complexity 4

## Marvel / DC

### A Day in Daily Bugle
You're a junior reporter chasing a Spider-Man story. Spider-Man barely appears. The story is about you, the city, and what you decide to print.
- **Pros:** Inverts expectations — superhero IP without superhero scenes. Stats *Deadline*, *Sources Burned*, *Truth*.
- **Cons:** Reader who came for capes may feel cheated; needs to commit to its angle.
- complexity 2

### Gotham, Off-Duty
A GCPD officer on leave who can't stop noticing things. One week, one neighbourhood, a slowly-revealing conspiracy. Batman is a rumour and a shadow.
- **Pros:** Constrained geography; the IP gives instant atmosphere; works as character study.
- **Cons:** Restraint required — Batman appearing would break the tone the premise is built on.
- complexity 3

## Sherlock Holmes

### The Apprentice's Casebook
You are a young Scotland Yard sergeant who occasionally consults Holmes. Three cases, each a scene-cluster, with Holmes as a high-stat NPC you can ask for help — at a cost to your standing.
- **Pros:** The "ask Holmes" mechanic as a flag-spend is perfect engine fit. Three discrete cases keeps scope honest.
- **Cons:** Mystery writing is hard; clue-tracking via flags can become a spreadsheet if not careful.
- complexity 4

## Studio Ghibli (Spirited Away / Mononoke register)

### The Bathhouse, A Year Later
A young human stays on at the spirit bathhouse to work off a debt. Episodic encounters with guests, slow understanding of who you are. Stats: *Name remembered*, *Trust of Yubaba*.
- **Pros:** Tonally the easiest fit with "engine serves the text" — quiet, mood-driven, scene-as-vignette. Intangible inventory (your name, a memory, a promise) is exactly Sunless Sea territory.
- **Cons:** Tone is delicate; one heavy-handed scene breaks it.
- complexity 3

## Cthulhu Mythos (Lovecraft, public-ish domain)

### The Innsmouth Letters
You inherit a house in Innsmouth in 1928. Each scene is a day; each day a letter, a visitor, a discovery. *Sanity*, *Knowledge*, *Suspicion of Townspeople*.
- **Pros:** Public-domain-adjacent so the IP problem is softer. Mythos is *built* for branching dread; sanity-as-stat is the original gamebook stat.
- **Cons:** The genre is so well-trodden it needs a fresh angle to not feel rote.
- complexity 3

## Best bets

- **Smuggler's Run on Tatooine** *(complexity 2)* — Single setting, single night, instantly readable to anyone. The constrained geography matches Fighting Fantasy's discipline, and Star Wars cantina texture does most of the atmospheric work for you. Authoring simplicity wins.
- **Waterdeep: One Night in the Yawning Portal** *(complexity 2)* — Hub-and-spoke is the Fabled Lands shape this engine was practically built for. D&D's named NPCs and factions give a small scene count outsized weight. Strong narrative-fidelity fit; resists the temptation to simulate combat.
- **A Day in Daily Bugle** *(complexity 2)* — High concept, low surface area. Superhero IP without superhero mechanics is exactly the kind of constraint that produces sharp writing. Engine serves the text; no temptation to bolt on powers.
- **The Bathhouse, A Year Later** *(complexity 3)* — Best tonal match for the inspirations list (Sunless Sea, point-and-click texture, Disco Elysium intangible inventory). Higher complexity because mood writing is hard, but the engine's quiet flag-and-flavour model fits like a glove.
- **The Innsmouth Letters** *(complexity 3)* — Mythos is closer to public domain, which softens the IP awkwardness. Sanity and dread are the original branching-fiction stats; the day-by-day structure gives clean act breaks without authoring sprawl.
