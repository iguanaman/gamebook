# Naming the Engine

## What We Have

A static-site gamebook engine: YAML scenes, branching choices, stats/flags, undo, audio narration, multi-story selector. Stories range from D&D-style dungeon crawls to kids' adventure games. The project folder is currently called `gamebook` — functional, generic, says nothing.

---

## The Gamebook Heritage Names

Names that nod directly to the genre — Fighting Fantasy, Choose Your Own Adventure, Lone Wolf. These signal lineage immediately to anyone who recognises the tradition.

**Leafbound**
A gamebook is a physical object you flip through; "leaf" is both a page and something organic that grows.
- Pros: warm, nostalgic without being retro, works equally for a kids' story and a dungeon crawl
- Cons: might read as "Leaflet"-adjacent (the mapping library); not immediately clear it's interactive
- complexity 1

**Turnpage**
Simple, direct — you turn pages, you make turns. Implies agency without overstating it.
- Pros: immediately legible to anyone who's read a gamebook
- Cons: sounds a bit product-y, like a SaaS name; no texture
- complexity 1

**Passages**
Borrowed from Twine's heritage (Twine calls its nodes "passages"). Double meaning: text passages and the passage through a story.
- Pros: elegant, literary, works for any genre
- Cons: extremely close to the Twine association, may confuse people who know Twine; already used by other tools
- complexity 1

**Branching**
Ultra-literal. Does exactly what it says.
- Pros: immediately legible, good for discoverability
- Cons: no personality; sounds like a dev tool, not an experience
- complexity 1

## The Atlas / Cartography Names

A gamebook is a map of choices. These names lean into the spatial metaphor — you navigate, you find paths, you get lost.

**Waymark**
A waymark is a trail marker — it tells you where you are on a longer journey.
- Pros: implies progress, persistence, and consequence without being heavy-handed; works for short and long stories
- Cons: slightly corporate; "waymark" is used in hiking/orienteering contexts which may confuse searches
- complexity 1

**Cartograph**
Making a map of a story as you move through it.
- Pros: cerebral, interesting, implies exploration and authorship simultaneously
- Cons: slightly opaque; "cartograph" isn't a common word; might read as pretentious
- complexity 1

**Crossroads**
Classic branching-narrative metaphor. Every scene is a crossroads.
- Pros: immediately understood, warm, slightly mythic
- Cons: extremely common; "Crossroads" is used by roughly one hundred other products
- complexity 1

**Pathfinder**
You find a path through the story.
- Pros: strong verb energy, implies agency
- Cons: completely owned by the tabletop RPG brand; legally and culturally off-limits
- complexity 1

## The Book / Library Names

Lean into the literary object — the book, the page, the binding, the library.

**Fablebook**
Fable + gamebook. Implies authored, fictional, slightly mythic stories.
- Pros: warm, memorable, genre-flexible; echoes "Fabled Lands" (one of our direct inspirations)
- Cons: Facebook's old name was "TheFacebook" which may trigger unwanted associations for some; "fable" implies fairy-tale register specifically
- complexity 1

**Inkbound**
Stories are ink on a page; you are bound to follow the thread (and "bound" = both constrained and headed somewhere).
- Pros: literary without being stiff; the double meaning of "bound" is productive; good for dark or serious stories
- Cons: "Inkbound" is a published indie game (deckbuilder RPG) — name collision, probably off-limits
- complexity 1

**Storybound**
You are bound into the story. Simple, warm.
- Pros: immediately legible; genre-neutral; works for kids' stories and dark fantasy equally
- Cons: very safe, possibly forgettable; "bound" suffix is overused in fantasy titles
- complexity 1

**Codex**
A codex is a bound manuscript — the precursor to the modern book. Implies age, weight, authority.
- Pros: single word, punchy, prestigious-feeling; works beautifully for D&D-adjacent content
- Cons: completely owned by the Anthropic product (Claude Codex); also used by many other tools; off-limits
- complexity 1

## The Thread / Narrative Flow Names

A story is a thread you follow. These names emphasise continuity, consequence, and the feel of narrative as something you move through.

**Threadline**
The through-line of a story, rendered as a thread you follow.
- Pros: feels modern and designed; works as a platform name; implies sequence and consequence
- Cons: slightly abstract; doesn't immediately read as "gamebook"
- complexity 1

**The Telling**
Stories are told. This foregrounds the act of narration — especially resonant given the audio feature.
- Pros: warm, oral-tradition feel; "telling" implies both the story and the telling of it; suits audio-first experiences
- Cons: article in the name is awkward for a product name; possessive form ("The Telling's...") is clunky
- complexity 1

**Storythread**
Simple compound. Follow the thread of the story.
- Pros: legible, warm, works for any genre
- Cons: compound words can feel clunky; "Storythread" sounds slightly generic
- complexity 1

**Recount**
To recount a story is to retell it. Also "recount" in the sense of going back (undo).
- Pros: clever double meaning that happens to match the undo feature; short and memorable
- Cons: "recount" primarily reads as vote-counting in current political climate; uphill battle for association
- complexity 1

## The Old-World / Tome Names

Lean into the fantasy register — parchment, tomes, scrolls, seals. More atmospheric, fits the D&D/Yawning Portal aesthetic, but may feel narrow for kids' stories.

**The Grimoire**
A grimoire is a book of magic — spells, secrets, and power.
- Pros: atmospheric, memorable, perfect for fantasy content
- Cons: locks the register firmly into dark fantasy; would feel wrong for a kids' Geometry Dash story
- complexity 1

**Folio**
A folio is a large-format book or manuscript. Implies the thing matters.
- Pros: elegant, short, slightly prestigious; works for any content
- Cons: very quiet — might not stick; also used by many companies (financial tools especially)
- complexity 1

**Lorehold**
A place where lore is held. Invented compound.
- Pros: original, invented words travel well; fantasy-adjacent without being locked to any genre
- Cons: "hold" suffix is heavily used in fantasy; "Lorehold" is a Magic: The Gathering college name — minor collision
- complexity 1

**Inkwell**
The well from which stories are drawn.
- Pros: warm, literary, familiar; evokes craft and authorship
- Cons: extremely common as a product name; many "Inkwell" apps already exist
- complexity 1

---

## The Action / Player-Verb Names

Name the act of playing, not the object. "You choose." "You turn." "You decide." These feel more interactive and modern.

**Turnwise**
You take your turn, wisely or not. A slight nod to Pratchett ("widdershins"/"turnwise").
- Pros: unusual, memorable, slightly playful; implies agency and consequence
- Cons: the Pratchett reference is too niche to land reliably; "wise" suffix can read as self-congratulatory
- complexity 1

**Chooser**
Ultra-direct. You are the chooser.
- Pros: completely legible; functional
- Cons: no texture; sounds like a UI component name, not a story engine
- complexity 1

**StoryPlayer**
Compound of what it is: a player for stories.
- Pros: completely clear for discoverability
- Cons: no character; sounds like a podcast app
- complexity 1

**Decider**
You decide.
- Pros: strong verb energy; short; implies real stakes
- Cons: slightly clinical; "The Decider" was a George W. Bush meme — unwanted baggage
- complexity 1

---

## The Invented / Coined Names

Completely original words. Riskier — harder to understand on first encounter — but more ownable and searchable.

**Yarnwise**
Yarnspinning + wise choices. Invented compound.
- Pros: warm, memorable, slightly whimsical; works for any genre; "yarn" is a storytelling word
- Cons: "Yarn" is a package manager — some dev confusion; "wise" suffix issue as above
- complexity 1

**Leafmark**
Page + waymark. Invented compound.
- Pros: original combination; works for both the book metaphor and the trail metaphor
- Cons: not immediately obvious; might read as a bookmarking app
- complexity 1

**Storykeep**
A keep as in a fortress; also "to keep" a story — to hold it, preserve it.
- Pros: invented, original; implies the stories have weight and permanence; fits fantasy register without being locked in
- Cons: "keep" might read as "keep playing" or as a savings product; slightly ambiguous
- complexity 1

**Fablepath**
Fable + path. You follow the path of a fable.
- Pros: combines two of the strongest metaphors in the space; genre-flexible if "fable" is read broadly
- Cons: compound words can land flat; "fable" register risk as above
- complexity 1

---

## Best Bets

**Fablebook** — Invented compound that does double work: names the genre (gamebook) and the tone (fable, authored fiction). Directly echoes "Fabled Lands," one of our core inspirations. Low friction for any genre if you read "fable" as "authored story" rather than "fairy tale." Authoring simplicity and narrative fidelity are baked into the name. Only risk: Facebook ghost.

**Waymark** — Short, modern, implies progress and consequence without fantasy baggage. The trail-marker metaphor maps well onto "you are making your way through a story; here is where you are." Works equally for dungeon crawl and kids' game. Authoring simplicity isn't encoded in the name but nothing fights it. Search collision with hiking terms is a real but manageable problem.

**Threadline** — Feels designed and modern in a way that suits a web-native tool. "Thread" carries both the narrative thread and the YAML-linked scene chain. Consequence and continuity are in the name. Doesn't immediately read as "gamebook" — you'd have to earn that association — but that's a positioning problem, not a naming problem.

**Storybound** — Safest choice. Immediately legible, warm, genre-neutral, no collisions. Works for kids and adults. Won't win a naming award but won't lose anyone either. The three pillars are all compatible with it; nothing about "storybound" fights the engine's philosophy.

**The Telling** — Best fit for the audio-forward experience. If narrated gamebooks become the signature feature, "The Telling" foregrounds exactly that — oral tradition, voice, the act of being told a story. Highest personality of any option here. Awkward as a product name (the article), but could be shortened to just "Telling" if needed.
