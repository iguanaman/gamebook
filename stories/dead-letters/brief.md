# Dead Letters — Story Brief

## Setting
Fictional American city (Harmon City), 1947. Post-war prosperity on the surface — new buildings going up, veterans coming home, city hall smiling for the papers. Underneath: rot. The war money got laundered into city contracts, and the men who run things learned in Europe that killing is easy.

## Hook
A woman walks into your office with a missing-husband case. Routine. Except the husband was a city surveyor who knew where the bodies — literal and financial — were buried. Three days later she's dead too, and you're the only one who knows the case is connected. What started as a missing persons job is now a thread that pulls at the whole city.

## Player Role
You are a private detective. Male, mid-40s, ex-cop (left under pressure, reasons unclear at start — a stat-gated reveal). You want the truth. What stands in your way: the city's power structure, your own compromised past, and the fact that the people who want this buried have badges and guns.

## Arc Shape
Classic Chinatown unraveling — you pull a thread, the conspiracy gets bigger, and by Act 3 you understand the full shape of it. The tragedy isn't that you don't solve it; it's what solving it costs. Personal stakes escalate: the client dies, someone you trust betrays you, the conspiracy reaches someone you thought was clean.

## Endings
Three endings, determined by evidence gathered (a stat) + a final binary moral choice:

1. **Expose** — high evidence + choose to go public. You bring it down. Career over, maybe your life too. The city changes, slowly.
2. **Bury It** — low evidence OR choose to walk away. You survive. Dirty. The conspiracy continues. The city keeps smiling.
3. **Martyr** — high evidence + the wrong people find out first. You die. But you left something behind — a package to a journalist. It gets out anyway, but you don't live to see it.

## Tone Reference
Chinatown (1974), The Maltese Falcon (1941). Morally compromised protagonist, institutions that cannot be redeemed, tragedy as the cost of integrity. Not campy pulp — earnest and bleak.

## Visual Feel
Broad mood: amber and cigarette smoke. Dark backgrounds, warm sepia/gold text, aged paper texture. Oppressive but beautiful. Exact palette and fonts decided in Stage 1.

## Narrator Voice
First-person hard-boiled interior monologue — the detective narrating his own case, past tense, like he's writing it up knowing how it ends. Dry, laconic, flashes of dark humor, never melodramatic.
