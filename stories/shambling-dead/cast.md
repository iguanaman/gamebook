# The Long Walk Out — Cast

## Dale Pearson (wounded neighbour, Act 1 companion)
**Voice:** `male_midlife_american_dark_tough` — midlife American, weary, with a hardness that fits a man who has worked outdoors for thirty years and is now bleeding on his own lawn. Reads as someone trying to sound steadier than he is.
**Body:** Sandy-grey stubble he hasn't shaved in two days. A wedding band he turns with his thumb when he doesn't know what to say. In Act 1 he holds his right thigh through his trouser; the cloth is darker than the rest, though he keeps the leg angled away from the player.
**Voice on the page:** Short sentences. Clipped, regional, lower-register. Uses the player's first name often — old neighbour habit. Avoids the words "bitten" and "infected"; substitutes "scraped it on the fence" or "caught it climbing." When pressed, deflects with a question or a half-joke about the weather. Never raises his voice; the closer to panic he is, the quieter he gets.
**Contradiction:** A practical man whose practicality is breaking down in real time. He has lived through real things — a tour, a wife who died last year — and is the person on the street who *should* know what to do. He doesn't. He's lying to himself before he lies to the player, and the player can hear it if they listen.
**Wants:** To not be alone when it happens. To be remembered as someone who didn't slow anyone down.
**Fears:** Being put down by a friend. Being seen turning. That the player will look at him the way he looked at the thing on the lawn.
**First seen:** Act 1 Beat 3 (front lawn).
**Recurs in:** Act 1 Beat 4-Hub (visibly deteriorating in framing prose), Beat 6 (route discussion), Beat 7 (the decision), `talk_dale` conversation hub. Carries forward into Act 2 only on Exits D, E, F.

## Caleb Pearson (terrified teen, Act 1 companion)
**Voice:** `male_young_american_weak` — young American, thin and high-pitched, hesitant; reads as a fifteen-year-old who hasn't slept and hasn't eaten and is trying not to cry on the phone. (Same surname as Dale by coincidence of street, not relation — the script does not state they are family; resolve in Stage 5 if it would distract.)
**Body:** Hooded sweatshirt zipped to the chin even though it's warm. A school lanyard he forgets is still around his neck. Picks at a scab on his left thumb constantly; it bleeds before the act ends.
**Voice on the page:** Half-formed sentences. Trails off. "I don't —" and "I mean —" twice per exchange. Uses "sir" or "ma'am" once, then catches himself. When he does commit to a sentence, it's either a question or an apology. Asks the player questions he already knows the answers to, because he wants the company of the words.
**Contradiction:** Reads as a passenger — the kid who will get the player killed — but is the only character in Act 1 who tells the player the truth without prompting. He saw his father turn. He says so. Dale won't. The frightened one is the honest one.
**Wants:** To be told what to do, and to not be lied to about it.
**Fears:** The dark. Being left in a building. That the player will eventually find an excuse to leave him too.
**First seen:** Act 1 Beat 4-Hub (upstairs window of the Pearson house) — passive sighting if not approached, sets `caleb_seen`. Active recruitment in Beat 5.
**Recurs in:** Act 1 Beat 6 (route discussion — quiet bark about the dark of the drain), Beat 7 (silent witness if Dale decision involves him). Carries forward into Act 2 on Exits C, E, F. Reappears in Act 2 if `caleb_seen` is set but he was not recruited (refused but alive — meets the player again on the route).

## Ruth Hollander (older woman in the church, Act 2 companion)
**Voice:** `female_elder_american_relatable_likeable` — older American woman, warm, plain-spoken, the kind of voice that makes a stranger feel like a neighbour inside a sentence. The warmth is real; what's *underneath* it is the work she's doing not to come apart. Distinct from Dale (`male_midlife_american_dark_tough`) and Caleb (`male_young_american_weak`); pairs cleanly with Wren and Hollis without clashing.
**Body:** Late sixties. Reading glasses on a chain she taps when she's listening. A cardigan she has not changed in three days, with a stain near the left cuff that is not coffee. Hands steady when she's making tea, less so when she's not. A wedding ring on a finger too thin for it now.
**Voice on the page:** Long sentences for a place this loud — she finishes them, even when other people are stopping mid-word at sounds. "Sweetheart" and "honey" used precisely, not casually. Asks the player questions back instead of answering — kind, not evasive. Uses the word "supper" instead of "dinner." Knows what a barricade is and how to build one and does not pretend she doesn't.
**Contradiction:** The warm grandmother who is feeding her dead husband. She has been keeping a lie for three days that costs her every hour she keeps it. The warmth is not a mask; the warmth is what is left after she chose to stop seeing what is in the side room. The careful player can hear the seam.
**Wants:** To take care of one more person before she stops. To not lie any more than she has to.
**Fears:** That the lie about her husband will become a lie about everyone else. That her son is dead in another state and she will never know. That she will be a burden on someone younger than she is.
**First seen:** Act 2 Beat 10 (the church door).
**Recurs in:** `talk_ruth` (Beat 10), Beat 11 (alley spotter on the Mara hard-way if recruited), Beat 13 (rest beat — small monologue about her son if Wren did not preempt it), Beat 14 (pharmacy alternative — her clinic key, +2 health one-shot if `ruth_key`). Carries into Act 3 if `elder_alive`.

## Wren (stranger on the road, Act 2 ally or threat)
**Voice:** `female_adult_american_droll_wry_dry` — American adult woman, dry, deflective, the kind of voice that has learned to make people like it as a survival skill. The dryness is the tell — she uses humour to step away from questions, and she uses it the same way every time. Distinct from Ruth's warmth, Mara's youth, the female narrators, and the male principals. Pairs against Hollis cleanly.
**Body:** Early thirties. Hair pulled back with whatever was on her wrist. A canvas bag on a long strap she keeps across her body, never on the ground; the bag has weight. A scab on the back of her hand that looks like she scratched it on purpose. Steady gaze; she does not flinch at sounds the way Caleb does, and the player should notice that.
**Voice on the page:** Short. Wry. Two-beat jokes — setup, punch — that are *too rehearsed* if the player is listening. She deflects the same way each time and the careful player notices the pattern. When honest, drops the dryness and speaks plainly — and that change is the *real* tell, not the lies. She lies about geography (the wrong direction), waves off the bag, and tells the truth about what she wants in a way that sounds like a confession because it is one.
**Contradiction:** A liar who is honest about being one if you ask the right way. She is not a predator — she is a person who has been alone for two weeks and decided, before meeting the player, that she would use them to get to the truck. Probing her is what makes her stop. She is the prototype for the brief's *paranoia keeps you alive, openness gets you the warm endings* tension: the player who probes (cynical) gets the truck, the player who trusts (warm) gets robbed, the player who refuses (over-cynical) gets neither, and the player who probes *and then chooses kindness* gets both.
**Wants:** To get to her brother's truck at the eastern edge. To not have to leave another person at a fence.
**Fears:** That the player will see through her before she has decided to come clean. That the truck won't be there. That she is becoming the thing she's afraid of.
**First seen:** Act 2 Beat 8 (apartments corridor, fire escape) — sets `met_wren`. Or Act 2 Beat 9 (apartments hub roof).
**Recurs in:** `talk_wren` (Beat 9 — three topics, each a tell), Beat 12 (silent presence at Hollis's camp — she reads the camp faster than the player and her body language is a *fourth* tell), Beat 13 (the truth-or-betrayal beat — load-bearing). Carries into Act 3 if `stranger_alive` AND not `betrayed_by_wren` (or Wren-leaves-with-supplies). Truck (`has_keys`) is gated on `probed_wren`.

## Hollis (leader of the hostile-survivors group, Act 2 antagonist)
**Voice:** `male_midlife_american_loud_confident_cocky` — American midlife man, loud where Dale is quiet, confident where Caleb is hesitant. The kind of voice that fills a tent. Distinct from Dale (`male_midlife_american_dark_tough`) and from any narrator voice. Pairs against Ruth and Wren without clash.
**Body:** Forties. Heavy-shouldered, the build of a man who used to do real work and now does less of it. A short beard kept neat — the *neatness* is the tell, in a city where nobody else has trimmed in two weeks. Boots on, always; boots that fit too well, because he took them off a body that was the right size. A handgun on the right hip, openly carried, and a wedding ring he did not earn.
**Voice on the page:** Sentences that are too long for a place this loud. He monologues — and he is the only person in Act 2 who *does*. The brief's contract on conversation pacing (clipped, listened-into) is broken on purpose around him: he talks like a man who has not been listened-into in a long time, because his perimeter holds. Charm-words: "friend," "neighbour," "we." Asks personal questions and remembers the answers. When he stops smiling the smile is the last thing to leave his face.
**Contradiction:** A man who is good at hosting in a world where hosting is a weapon. Genuinely competent — the camp is well-built, his people are fed, the wire works. He is *not* a thug; he is what a community organiser becomes when the community has to eat. The horror is that he would have been a fine neighbour two weeks ago, and may even know it. He does not enjoy hurting people; he is past the point where that mattered.
**Wants:** To keep his perimeter holding through the winter. To take what he needs from people who will let him talk first.
**Fears:** Being seen for what he is by someone whose opinion he respects (the careful player is, briefly, that person — and he covers for it loudly). That his second will leave. That the loop on the radio will stop working.
**First seen:** Act 2 Beat 12 (the hostile-survivors camp).
**Recurs in:** Beat 12 fight / flee / yield resolution. If the player flees, his name is heard once more — on the radio, looped, the night of Beat 13. If the player fights and kills him, `killed_survivor` is set and his name closes the beat. Carries into Act 3 only as flag-driven memory; he does not survive the beat alive.

## Mara (the rescued child, Act 2/3 centrepiece)
**Voice:** `female_young_american_sweet_youthful_clear` — young American girl, ~7yo, clear and small and not yet exhausted-sounding. Distinct from every other principal; no clash with the female narrators, Ruth, or Wren.
**Body:** Seven. Hair in two uneven plaits she did herself two days ago. A jacket too big for her — her mother's, probably. Shoes on the wrong feet; she has not noticed and does not want to be told. Holds the player's hand or the strap of the player's bag, never her own pockets.
**Voice on the page:** Short questions in series. "Are you —" "Did you —" "Do you know —". Names her mother once and then does not until the player asks. Repeats things back to confirm them, the way small children do when they are afraid the adult will change their mind. Asks about the dark before she asks about anything else. Sleeps in the time it takes to close a door.
**Contradiction:** The hardest-earned save in the run, and the most patient passenger in the city. She does not slow the party meaningfully — she is small, quiet, and walks where she is told. The cost of having her is *bond* — every choice the player makes around her is read by her, and lying to her costs more than lying to an adult does. She is the brief's load — the player is no longer the person they were, and that is measured in how they speak to her.
**Wants:** Her mother. To not be put down somewhere she cannot see the player. To be told the truth, even when it's hard, the way her mother used to.
**Fears:** The dark. Being left in a building (echo of Caleb — by design). That the player will turn into one of *them* the way the man on the lawn did.
**First seen:** Act 2 Beat 11 (the supply closet, fourth floor of the crying building). Crying audible from Beat 11's street-level approach.
**Recurs in:** `talk_mara` (three topics — name, mother, dark — last one held for Act 3). Carries into Act 3 if `child_rescued` AND `child_alive`. Centre of E3, E5, E6 endings.

## Vance (leader of the dock crew, Act 3 antagonist)
**Voice:** `female_adult_american_sultry_villainous` — adult American woman, low-pitched, controlled, the kind of voice that does not raise itself because it has not needed to in a long time. Distinct from Ruth's warmth, Wren's dryness, Mara's youth, and the female narrators. Pairs against Hollis (`male_midlife_american_loud_confident_cocky`) by *contrast* — where he hosts, she negotiates; where he fills a tent, she empties a room.
**Body:** Late thirties / early forties. A high-vis vest she has cut down at the hem so it hangs at the hip — the marina's, repurposed. Hair pinned up with a pencil. A ring of keys on a carabiner at the right hip; one key hangs separate on a lanyard around her neck (the cruiser's — the *honest tell* that she is the gatekeeper). A clipboard always — laminated pages, dry-erase marker, a column for headcount and a column for supply-load. Boots that fit. A handgun she has not had to draw this week, though the holster is unbuttoned.
**Voice on the page:** Sentences sized for the work. She does not monologue — Hollis monologued; Vance does not. Three-question rhythm at first contact (who, how many, what's in the bag) and the rhythm does not vary per visitor. Names prices in numbers. Uses the word "math" twice in the player's hearing. Says "friend" once and only once and the player should notice it is the *only* warm word she uses. When she is honest, the dryness drops; she does not flinch at saying what she is doing. The honesty is not a tell — the steadiness *is*. She does not cover.
**Contradiction:** A community organiser whose community has run out of room. She is *not* a sadist — she does not enjoy the body on the floating dock. She made a decision two days ago about who the marina would let leave and she has not deviated from it since. The horror is that the math works: her perimeter has held longer than anyone else's, her people are fed, her crossings have been clean. Her competence is her cruelty. She is what Hollis would have become with another week of attrition and one more loss.
**Wants:** To get her own people across to the cove downriver before the marina goes under. To keep the math holding — clean books, clean math, clean record of who paid what.
**Fears:** That her second is reading her clipboard. That the loop on the radio is the wrong one and she has been calling the wrong frequency for two days. That she will find herself on the other side of her own gate one day and the next Vance will be waiting with the same three questions.
**First seen:** Act 3 Beat 15 (the marina gate) if `dock_crew_logged`; Beat 16 (the office) regardless.
**Recurs in:** `talk_vance` (Beat 16 — three topics, each a tell), Beat 17 (perimeter breach — her hidden join-the-crew offer fires here for the player who held the breach without being logged), Beat 18 (the climax — she names the cruiser's price out loud). She does not survive Act 3 in any ending — she leaves on the cruiser if the player did not take it (off-page death later, narration may reference), or she dies at the dock if the player did. Carries into endings only as flag-driven closing-narration line ("the woman with the clipboard, [outcome]").

## Tomás (lone-group leader at the seawall, Act 3 ally or threat)
**Voice:** `male_elder_american_wise_approachable` — older American man, weathered, plain-spoken, the kind of voice that has talked people through hard things at work for forty years. Distinct from Dale (`male_midlife_american_dark_tough`) by *register* — Dale's hardness was a man trying to sound steadier than he was; Tomás *is* steady, and his calm is the discomforting thing. Distinct from Ruth's warmth (`female_elder_american_relatable_likeable`) and from any narrator voice. Pairs cleanly with Vance (woman / man, predator / non-predator).
**Body:** Mid-sixties. A windbreaker over a denim shirt, both clean enough that the player notices — he has been near water and has rinsed. Reading glasses pushed up on grey hair. A wedding band on a finger that still fits. Two adults at his back at the seawall — a younger woman who watches without speaking, and a man with a bandaged arm who does not look at the player at all. He carries no weapon visibly, which is its own tell — he is past the point where a visible weapon would help him.
**Voice on the page:** Long sentences when he has time, short when he doesn't, and he knows the difference. Names the player's headcount correctly without being told (the way Hollis did, but he uses the read to *help*, not to count). Asks the player a question and listens to the answer; does not interrupt; does not finish the player's sentences. When he names a price, he names it the same way both times — no adjustment to test the player. Says "we" about his group and "you" about the player's, and never confuses the two.
**Contradiction:** A man who reads the player accurately and chooses kindness anyway, in a world where reading accurately and choosing kindness has become a luxury good. He is not a saint — his price is real, he will not give the kayak away, and he will leave the player behind if the player refuses to cooperate (`refused_tomas` locks the boathouse coop, and Tomás will not reopen the door at the climax). But he is the run's *contrast figure* to Vance: the answer to "what does competence look like when it does not compress into cruelty." For the player who survived Hollis and Wren, he is the third reading — the stranger whose tells point to *honesty*, and the player who has been trained on liars may misread him by reflex. Misreading him costs the kayak; reading him correctly opens E5 / E6.
**Wants:** To get his own three across the river to the cove. To not have to leave anyone at the seawall. To do this without ending up holding a clipboard.
**Fears:** That his read of the player is wrong this time. That the man with the bandaged arm is bitten and is hiding it. That he will run out of road before he runs out of decisions.
**First seen:** Act 3 Beat 16 (the seawall, marina hub).
**Recurs in:** `talk_tomas` (Beat 16 — three topics, third is a moral mirror), Beat 17 (boathouse coop — he is the door for the player who probed him; he and his group are *in* the boathouse if `boathouse_coop` fires; he is dead in the boathouse if `boathouse_loud` fires and the player went in late), Beat 18 (the kayak option — he runs the ferry math with the player). He does not become a companion-alive flag and does not appear in any ending image — his group leaves on the kayak before or after the player, on their own clock. The narration may reference his outcome in closing prose if `read_tomas` was set ("the man at the seawall — [outcome]").

## Soldier with the Megaphone (checkpoint, Act 2 underpass corridor)
**Voice:** `male_young_american_forthright` — young American man, plain, the kind of voice that has been told what to say and is running out of belief in it. Distinct from Caleb's `male_young_american_weak` by *register* — Caleb is hesitant, this soldier is over-rehearsed. No clash with Dale, Hollis, Tomás, or any narrator.
**Body:** Late twenties. Body armour over a uniform that fits worse than it did a week ago. Hands shake on the megaphone — visible to the careful player from across the wire. A radio behind him on a sandbag, looping one phrase. A body has been in the concertina wire long enough that flies have settled. The second soldier at his shoulder does not speak.
**Voice on the page:** Three sentences, repeated. "Show your hands. Do not approach the wire. We are not authorised to admit." The first time he says them they are training; the third time the player hears them they are something else. When he breaks the loop — only once, only at the third exchange, only to the patient — he uses ten words ("take this, go east, do not come back through here").
**Contradiction:** A man whose authority is the only thing keeping him upright, who is about to give it away to a stranger he likes the look of. His patience-reward is breaking the rule he has been holding for two days.
**Wants:** To go home. To not have to shoot the next person who pushes the wire. To have one good thing happen before the radio finally stops.
**Fears:** That the radio loop will outlast him. That the body in the wire was someone he knew. That the second soldier is reading him.
**First seen:** Act 2 Beat 8 (`checkpoint_approach`).
**Recurs in:** `checkpoint_wait_1`, `checkpoint_wait_2`, `checkpoint_wait_3`, `checkpoint_speak_1`, `death_d2_checkpoint`. Does not appear after Beat 8 — the underpass corridor's encounter is one-shot.

## Lone Walker (random encounter, Act 2 corridors)
**Voice:** `male_adult_african-american_uneducated` — adult, plain-spoken, the voice of someone who has been talking to nobody for a week and is rusty. Distinct from every principal; pairs cleanly with Wren if she is present in the apartments-corridor variant.
**Body:** Mid-thirties. A canvas duffel slung across his back, the strap reinforced with bicycle tubing. A water-bottle on his hip he refills from a marked jerry-can he is *not* carrying — meaning his stash is somewhere walkable. Three days of beard. He flinches at sounds the player has stopped hearing.
**Voice on the page:** Short sentences when the player approaches. Names the trade he wants before naming his own name. Says "thank you" twice — once for the warning, once for the trade — and means both. Will not stand still long enough to ask his name back.
**Contradiction:** A non-predator in a city that has begun rewarding predation. The player who has been trained to read tells will look for them and find none — that is itself the test the brief talks about, the third reading after Wren and Hollis where the answer is *honesty*. The player who walks past is paying a small bond cost; the player who trades or warns is being shown that not every stranger is a Wren.
**Wants:** Painkillers, water, news, anything. To not be the next body found by a service road.
**Fears:** That the player has a gun. That the next sound he flinches at is the one he should have run from. That his stash is gone when he gets back.
**First seen:** Act 2 Beat 8 random encounter (`random_lone_walker` or `random_lone_walker_apartments`).
**Recurs in:** `random_walker_trade` / `random_walker_trade_apartments`. Does not recur — one-shot per run, mutually exclusive with the other random encounters.

## Reece (Hollis's second, Act 2 hostile-survivors camp)
**Voice:** `male_adult_american_witty_casual` — adult, casual, dry. Distinct from Hollis's `male_midlife_american_loud_confident_cocky` by being *quiet* in the same room as Hollis — the voice of a man who has stopped trying to compete for the air. No clash with Dale, the soldier, or Tomás (Act 3).
**Body:** Late thirties. Lean where Hollis is heavy. Always in motion at the perimeter — pulling dead off the wire, never standing for a conversation. A wedding ring he turns the same way Dale does (Stage 5 may surface this if the player is reading carefully — Reece had a Dale-life before this). Eyes do not track Hollis when Hollis speaks.
**Voice on the page:** Two-word answers when addressed by the player. Three-word answers when addressed by Hollis, and the third word is always slower than the first two. Never says the player's name. Says "we" about Hollis's people the way a man says it about a job he is leaving.
**Contradiction:** Hollis's most competent person and his next mutiny. He has not decided yet, but the careful player can read the not-deciding. He is the wince-tell character — Stage 5 surfaces him in `talk_hollis_camp_topic` when Hollis names a perimeter number that is wrong.
**Wants:** To be done with this camp. To not have to do whatever Hollis is going to ask him to do tomorrow.
**Fears:** That Hollis will read him reading the camp. That he is already past the point where he could leave alone.
**First seen:** Act 2 Beat 12 (`hostile_funnel`).
**Recurs in:** `hostile_camp_read`, `hostile_camp`, `talk_hollis_camp`, `talk_hollis_camp_topic`, `hostile_resolution`. If the player flees, his last sighting is at the perimeter as they leave; if the player fights, he is the one Hollis loses control of first; if the player yields, he is the one whose face the player remembers from the slow death scene.

## Brick (Hollis's lieutenant, Act 2 hostile-survivors camp)
**Voice:** `male_midlife_american_rich_smooth_rugged` — midlife, rugged, the kind of voice you would buy a truck from. Distinct from Hollis (different *texture* — Hollis fills a tent; Brick fills a corner). Reused only as a one-scene bit-part — no recurring lines required, but assigned a voice for the rare bark the scene writer may give him.
**Body:** Forties. Heaviest of Hollis's people. Eyes on the player's bag the entire encounter. Boots that fit. A knife on the right hip, a pistol on the left, both worn enough that they have been used recently. He does not pull dead off the wire — Reece does that; Brick stands.
**Voice on the page:** Bit-part. May not speak at all in Stage 5, or may say one line ("show me what's in there"). If he does speak, his voice is unhurried — the same comfort Hollis has, but without the charm. The player should read him as the one who would not have been argued with.
**Contradiction:** Comfortable in the camp. Has not lost anything yet. The player who reads Reece's wince and Brick's comfort is reading the camp's two halves correctly — Reece is leaving, Brick is staying, and Hollis does not know which of them is the bigger problem.
**Wants:** What the player has in the bag. To stop pretending Hollis's monologues are interesting.
**Fears:** Almost nothing visible. (That is its own tell.)
**First seen:** Act 2 Beat 12 (`hostile_funnel`).
**Recurs in:** `hostile_pass_attempt` (tails the player at distance), `hostile_camp` (background), `hostile_resolution`. One-scene bit-part; no carry-over.

## Distant Screams (off-page texture, Act 1)
**Voice:** No assigned TTS voice — these are *narrated* sounds, not lines. Listed here so the scene writer treats them as a consistent off-page presence: never named, never sourced to a specific person, always at middle distance. They are the brief's "humans-as-threat is offscreen in Act 1" made audible.
**Body:** None. Cul-de-sac, two streets over, the gas-station forecourt across the arterial. A scream cut short. A man shouting a child's name and not getting an answer. Glass breaking inside a house with the lights still on.
**Voice on the page:** Never quoted. Always summarised — "a scream, then nothing," "someone is shouting a name." The player should never be sure whether the off-page voice was a person being attacked or a person doing the attacking; that ambiguity is the point.
**Contradiction:** N/A — they are texture, not character. Listed for discipline.
**First seen:** Act 1 Beat 1.
**Recurs in:** Every beat of Act 1 at least once. Drops out of Act 2 — by then the player is among the survivors and the off-page becomes on-page.
